Exploring Mental Health

The Hardest Years

Depression and anxiety rates are highest among 10- to 20-year-olds. Around 60% of teenagers experience depression and 35% experience suicidal thoughts. This makes suicide the fourth leading cause of death in adolescence. Within these years teenagers are exposed to adversity, physical changes, hormone changes, pressure from peers, social media, etc. This is packed onto the childhood trauma most have already dealt with by this age. These changes can affect decision making, grades/school, and both mental and physical health.

Social Media

Social media is proven to be one of the worst things for your mental health. Teenagers will compare themselves to everything they see even though it is all edited to perfection. This is the leading cause of eating disorders. Or when they post something, it is common to get hate or bad feedback from their peers. However, phones also make teenagers feel less alone if they are connected digitally. Overall, it can be a healthy form of communication and even escapism but only in moderation.

Watching for Signs

Teens will often give signs of depression, but some will hide it better than others whether they are trying to or not. The main signs to look out for are constant sleeping, tough time concentrating, appetite change, loss of interest in activities, irritability, anger, lethargic, drinking/drug use, harmful behaviors, drop in grades, and many more. Moving forward depends on the severity of the issues. But statistics show that most teenagers drastically improved after mental health counseling and/or medication.

Finding A Therapist

Whether it is finding a therapist for you or somebody else, it can be a frustrating process. Thankfully, there are resources available that check your location, financial status, and find exactly what you are looking for. This allows you to view their profiles and when you find the right one, set up an appointment. However, it might take a few sessions to find your perfect match.
